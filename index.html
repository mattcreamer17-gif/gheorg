Opening

Gheorg launched in 2020 out of Sydney with a clear idea: put three of the best-evidenced therapy approaches (ACT, CBT, positive psychology) in front of kids in short daily doses, with a robot that makes the experience feel safe. Five years in, that idea has the APA Seal of Acceptance, 14,000 families, a US headquarters in Florida, and a Top 100 spot at eMerge Americas 2026.

The next twelve months look different from the first five. Schools, pediatric clinics, and health plans want answers to questions that parents never ask: data governance, outcomes evidence, where AI lives in the product, who reviews new content, how fast new modules can ship without diluting the clinical integrity that got you the APA Seal in the first place.

The frame

A quick way to think about Gheorg's stack. Roughly 60% of the work underneath the app (content management, subscription plumbing, parent progress views, HSA/FSA workflows, school-district licensing) is traditional code and database work. Roughly 30% is rule-based logic (check-in scoring, escalation triggers, content pacing). Roughly 10% is genuinely AI work (personalisation of exercise selection, early-warning signal detection, language adaptation).

Most small teams shipping into regulated spaces waste budget putting LLMs where Postgres would do the job faster, cheaper, and with better review trails. The more useful question is where the review bottleneck sits, and what belongs on which layer.

The relevant case study

Feeld, a product company with a sensitive user population and a small team, ran a scoped sprint with Eduba: a workshop with the leadership team, advisory calls, an Organizational Context Architecture, and a Strategic Operations Framework. The deliverable was not a tool. It was a map of how decisions and content should flow inside the company, so the CTO could add features without rewriting the operating model each time.

Gheorg sits in the same shape.
